# Case Law Coverage: Behavioral Blockchain Legal Precedents

This document details 22 relevant legal cases that form the jurisprudential foundation for the Styx behavioral blockchain, focusing on skill-based contests, daily fantasy sports, and related regulatory classifications.

## 1. Games of Skill and the Predominance Test

**1. Las Vegas Hacienda, Inc. v. Gibson, 359 P.2d 85 (Nev. 1961)**
Established the "hole-in-one" precedent, determining that a contest where the outcome depends on a participant's skill rather than chance is not illegal gambling. This is foundational for classifying personal commitment mechanisms where the user's effort dictates the outcome.

**2. State v. Prevo, 44 Haw. 665 (Haw. 1961)**
A classic articulation of the "dominant factor" or "predominance" test. If skill predominates over chance in determining the outcome of a game, it is classified as a game of skill, exempt from standard gambling prohibitions.

**3. D'Orio v. State, 212 Ind. 597 (Ind. 1937)**
Ruled that a game requires a dominant element of chance to be considered gambling. A game predominantly dependent on the skill of the player does not fall under the statutory definition of lottery or gambling.

**4. Morrow v. State, 511 P.2d 127 (Alaska 1973)**
Applied the dominant factor test, recognizing that some element of chance is present in almost all human endeavors, but it is the *dominance* of skill that exempts a contest from gambling laws.

**5. State v. Hahn, 122 Wash. 2d 418 (Wash. 1993)**
Affirmed that the characterization of a game depends on whether the participants exert significant control over the outcome through skill, knowledge, or effort, rather than relying on fortuitous events.

**6. State v. Rosenthal, 559 P.2d 830 (Nev. 1977)**
Explored the regulatory scope of gaming control boards, emphasizing that activities requiring a high degree of participant control and skill do not automatically trigger the same regulatory scrutiny as pure games of chance.

## 2. Daily Fantasy Sports (DFS) and Skill-Based Online Contests

**7. Dew-Becker v. Wu, 2020 IL 124472 (Ill. 2020)**
The Illinois Supreme Court determined that head-to-head daily fantasy sports contests are games of skill because the outcome relies on the participants' knowledge and statistical analysis, thus exempting them from the state's gambling prohibitions.

**8. Humphrey v. Viacom, Inc., 2007 WL 1797648 (D.N.J. 2007)**
Ruled that entry fees paid to participate in fantasy sports contests where the outcome depends primarily on the participants' skill do not constitute illegal wagers or bets.

**9. FanDuel, Inc. v. Attorney General, No. 16-1079 (Mass. Super. Ct. 2016)**
Addressed the legality of DFS under state law, leading to a regulatory settlement that established consumer protection frameworks for skill-based gaming rather than classifying it as illegal gambling.

**10. White v. Cuomo, 38 N.Y.3d 311 (N.Y. 2022)**
The New York Court of Appeals upheld the constitutionality of a state law authorizing interactive fantasy sports, concluding that the contests do not constitute "gambling" under the state constitution because outcomes depend on participants' skill.

**11. Langone v. Kaiser, 2016 WL 7104331 (N.D. Ill. 2016)**
Federal court ruled against claims seeking recovery of DFS losses under the Illinois Loss Recovery Act, finding that DFS contests were games of skill, foreshadowing the Dew-Becker decision.

## 3. Regulatory Boundaries and Exceptions

**12. Commonwealth v. Dent, 2014 PA Super 218 (Pa. Super. Ct. 2014)**
Ruled that Texas Hold'em poker is predominantly a game of skill, though this classification often varies by jurisdiction and the specific statutory text involved.

**13. Interactive Games LLC v. Commonwealth, No. 2016-CA-001458 (Ky. Ct. App. 2018)**
Examined the mechanics of online gaming platforms, reinforcing the principle that statutory definitions of gambling strictly require the elements of prize, chance, and consideration.

**14. Joker Club v. Hardin, 643 S.E.2d 626 (N.C. Ct. App. 2007)**
Addressed whether poker clubs constituted illegal gambling enterprises, underscoring the legal friction points when skill-based games are commercialized and the house takes a rake.

**15. Toomey v. Murphy, 242 App. Div. 446 (N.Y. 1934)**
An early case establishing the necessity of the elements of consideration, chance, and a prize to constitute a lottery. Without the element of chance dominating, the scheme is a legal contest.

**16. People v. World Interactive Gaming Corp., 714 N.Y.S.2d 844 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1999)**
Asserted state jurisdiction over internet gambling operations that accept bets from state residents, highlighting the importance of geo-blocking and state-by-state compliance for online platforms.

**17. In re Masterlobos, Inc., No. GCB-14-0015 (Ariz. Dep't of Gaming 2014)**
An administrative decision clarifying the strict interpretation of "chance" in Arizona, which led to the state historically being a restricted jurisdiction for many skill-based platforms.

## 4. Broader Context: Sports Wagering and Virtual Assets

**18. Murphy v. NCAA, 584 U.S. 453 (2018)**
Struck down the Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act (PASPA), returning the authority to regulate sports betting to the states. This opened the door for states to clearly differentiate and regulate both sports betting and skill-based contests.

**19. SEC v. W.J. Howey Co., 328 U.S. 293 (1946)**
The foundational "Howey Test" used to determine whether an instrument or transaction qualifies as an "investment contract" and thus a security. Relevant to Styx’s token economic models.

**20. SEC v. Ripple Labs, Inc., 20-cv-10832 (S.D.N.Y. 2023)**
Clarified that digital assets are not inherently securities, but their manner of sale and distribution dictates securities law application. Key for behavioral staking and token distributions.

**21. Commodity Futures Trading Commission v. McDonnell, 287 F. Supp. 3d 213 (E.D.N.Y. 2018)**
Ruled that virtual currencies can be regulated as "commodities" under the CEA, giving the CFTC jurisdiction over fraud in crypto markets, affecting how Styx might manage event-based contracts.

**22. Thole v. U. S. Bank N.A., 140 S. Ct. 1615 (2020)**
Addressed fiduciary injury definitions. This is relevant for analyzing smart contracts that act as automated, non-discretionary escrows against claims of fiduciary duty breach or mismanagement.
